Case Study: FlowTROLL Setup with VuSitu

Why UX Writing Can't Wait Until the End

Project Details

FlowTROLL is a doppler sensor that measures the velocity of water flowing through a pipe or channel. It's a product originally developed by an Australian company that In-Situ acquired in 2018. In-Situ wanted to make sensor setup easier, so much easier that users could do it with a mobile app.

Some of the advanced settings are confusing. My job was to explain them in plain language. Most FlowTROLL users aren't scientists and don't have technical backgrounds.

The Challenge

Unfortunately, this was one of those projects that began with someone saying, "We need some text in these wireframes." The product manager and UX designer gave me a brief run-through of the UI and why the advanced settings needed explanation. The conversation ended with, "Talk to so-and-so for more information. He's the engineer on this, so he can tell you how the settings work." I knew I was in for a lot of hard work just trying to understand sensor setup.

Step 1: Research

The first thing I had to do was read the product manual.

Step 2: Simplify

Next, I attempted to simplify the relevant content and put it into words any reader could understand.

Step 3: Review

I reviewed the text with the product owner and engineers.

Step 4: Iterate

Getting accurate text was a process of revision and review with the engineers.

The Results

The final messages explain the advanced settings in clear language. But most people who use them will have to contact the technical support team for guidance. That's not great UX, and nothing I can write can make up for the software's inability to guide users through sensor configuration.

So what could we do better? A step-by-step walkthrough of the advanced settings might help. But the biggest improvement we could make would be to involve the UX writer as early as possible. Ideally, we'd use a structured approach that involved the following:

  • Understanding users, their needs, and their pain points.
  • Stating the problem we wanted to solve in clear language.
  • Coming up with several potential solutions.
  • Building a prototype of our best idea.
  • Testing the prototype and tweaking the solution as necessary.

1. Conduct Foundational Research

Interview users. Create personas, journey maps, and user stories.

2. Define the Problem

For example: John is a wheat farmer with no technical background who needs an easy way to set up his doppler sensors because the state requires him to monitor his water usage.

3. Brainstorm Solutions

Sketch ideas. Use a mind map. Make wireframes.

4. Prototype and Test

Build a working prototype. Let users test it. Observe the test and change things accordingly.
